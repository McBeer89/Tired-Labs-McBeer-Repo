# Ransomware threat landscape targeting financial services: March 2026

**Financial services faces its most complex ransomware threat environment ever, with 65% of firms reporting attacks in 2024 and attack volumes doubling into 2025.** Despite record-low payment rates (20–28%) and major law enforcement disruptions, the ecosystem grew more fragmented and dangerous: **126–141 active ransomware groups** operated in 2025, up from roughly 70 in 2023, with Qilin, Akira, and Clop emerging as the dominant threats to financial institutions (Emsisoft, "State of Ransomware in the U.S. 2025"; Picus Security, "Top 10 Ransomware Groups of 2025"). The financial sector recorded **451 ransomware cases** in 2025 and **$365.6 million in reported ransom payments** from 432 incidents between 2022–2024, second only to manufacturing (Check Point, "2025 Finance Sector Landscape Report"; FinCEN, "Financial Trend Analysis on Ransomware," December 2025). For a wealth management firm of this profile—52,000 employees, 14,500 branch offices, $1.8T AUM—the convergence of supply chain attacks, infostealer-to-ransomware pipelines, and escalating regulatory obligations creates a threat surface that demands both tactical and strategic intelligence investment.

## 1. Active ransomware groups confirmed targeting financial services

The ransomware ecosystem underwent severe fragmentation in 2025. RansomHub, Black Basta, 8Base, BianLian, and Cactus all ceased operations between January and April 2025, while displaced affiliates migrated to surviving and new operations, driving a **23% increase in extortion attacks** to 6,182 globally (Symantec/Broadcom Threat Hunter Team, "Ransomware 2026" report, January 2026). Flashpoint documented **406 publicly disclosed financial sector ransomware victims** from April 2024 through April 2025, approximately 7% of all ransomware listings (Flashpoint, "Top Threat Actor Groups Targeting Financial Sector," 2025).

**Qilin** emerged as the most prolific operation of 2025 with **946 victims by year-end**, including 69 confirmed finance-sector targets among 590 business attacks by October (Comparitech data via Industrial Cyber, October 2025; Picus Security, 2025). Qilin's September 2025 "Korean Leaks" campaign—compromising MSP GJTec to breach **28 South Korean asset management firms** and exfiltrate over 1 million files—represents the archetype of MSP-facilitated cascading financial sector risk (The Hacker News, November 2025; Bitdefender research; Korea JoongAng Daily, September 23, 2025). Separately, Qilin stole **2.5 TB from Habib Bank AG Zurich** (Breached.company, late 2025). Bitdefender linked the Korean campaign to potential North Korean state-affiliated involvement via Moonstone Sleet.

**Akira** ranked second with approximately **717–740 leak site postings** and an estimated $244 million in total extortion revenue (Symantec/Broadcom, 2026; Picus Security, 2025). Its August 14, 2025, attack on Marquis Software Solutions—a vendor serving 700+ banks and credit unions—compromised **over 400,000 consumers across 74+ institutions** via CVE-2024-40766 in SonicWall VPN (American Banker, "Seven Largest Banking Data Breaches of 2025"). Flashpoint attributed **34 financial sector victims** to Akira from April 2024 to April 2025.

**Clop** continued its signature pattern of mass zero-day exploitation, adding 500+ victims in 2025 through campaigns against Cleo file transfer products (CVE-2024-50623/CVE-2024-55956, December 2024 onward) and Oracle E-Business Suite (CVE-2025-61882, July–November 2025). Financial sector victims included **Western Alliance Bank** (21,899 customers, SSNs stolen via Cleo exploitation) and multiple Oracle EBS users processing payroll, financial ledgers, and HR files (SOCRadar, "Top 10 CVEs of 2025"; Google Cloud Blog, October 2025; BankInfoSecurity, October 7, 2025). Clop demanded up to **$50 million from individual organizations** in the Oracle campaign.

**RansomHub** led all groups in financial sector targeting with **38 confirmed financial victims** from April 2024 to April 2025 (Flashpoint, 2025) before ceasing operations on April 1, 2025, with DragonForce claiming the group migrated to its infrastructure (Malwarebytes/ThreatDown, April 2025; Bitsight, 2025). **Medusa** received a joint CISA/FBI/MS-ISAC advisory (AA25-071A, March 12, 2025) after exceeding 300 victims, with demands ranging from **$100,000 to $15 million** (Symantec, 2025). Notably, Symantec discovered North Korean Lazarus Group actors deploying Medusa ransomware against Middle Eastern financial institutions (Symantec/Security.com, "North Korean Lazarus Group Now Working With Medusa"). **Play** remained consistently active throughout 2025 among the top five groups, having compromised approximately 900 entities since mid-2022 (The Hacker News, June 2025).

**LockBit** attempted recovery after Operation Cronos with LockBit 4.0 (February 2025) and 5.0 (September 2025), but suffered a second infrastructure breach in May 2025 exposing affiliate details (Acronis TRU analysis; Wikipedia). Affiliate fees dropped to just **$500** to compete (Breached.company, "State of Ransomware 2026"). Key developer **Rostislav Panev** was extradited to the U.S. after arrest in Israel (S-RM Intelligence, March 2025; U.S. DOJ). **Black Basta** collapsed in January 2025, with 200,000 internal messages leaked on February 11, 2025, by "ExploitWhispers" (Intel 471; ReliaQuest). German federal police publicly identified **Oleg Evgenievich Nefedov** as key leader "GG" (Barracuda Networks, February 2026). Former members migrated to Cactus and SafePay groups.

Among **new groups**, DragonForce introduced a "cartel model" in April 2025 allowing affiliates to operate under their own branding, claiming 200+ victims (Symantec, 2026; SOCRadar). SafePay surged to the top group by May 2025 with 58 claimed victims that month (Cyble, May 2025). Hunters International rebranded as **World Leaks** (data-theft-only), targeting a third-party supplier of **UBS** in June 2025 and publishing data on 130,000 UBS employees (Infosecurity Magazine, July 2025; Group-IB, April 2025). In total, **57 new ransomware groups and 27 new extortion groups** emerged in 2025 (Cyble, "10 New Ransomware Groups of 2025").

## 2. TTPs at procedure-level detail across the kill chain

### Initial access: VPN exploitation and stolen credentials dominate

Vulnerability exploitation is the leading initial infection vector at **33% of cases** (Mandiant M-Trends 2025, Google Cloud Blog, April 2025), while compromised credentials account for **41% of root causes** (Sophos, "2025 Active Adversary Report," April 2025). Edge and VPN device exploitation jumped from **3% to 22%** of all exploitation cases between 2023 and 2024 (Verizon DBIR 2025). Barracuda found **90% of ransomware incidents** exploited firewalls via CVE or vulnerable account, with the fastest observed attack chain completing breach-to-encryption in **3 hours** and lateral movement in **10 minutes** (Barracuda Managed XDR 2025 Threat Report, Help Net Security, February 2026).

Stolen credentials gained through infostealers fuel this access economy. MFA was absent in **59% of cases** in Sophos data (Sophos, "2026 Active Adversary Report," covering November 2024–October 2025), and **67.32% of root causes were identity-related**. CrowdStrike reports **75% of initial access attempts are malware-free**, relying on credentials and identity misuse (CrowdStrike, 2025 Global Threat Report).

**ClickFix social engineering surged 517% in 2025**, becoming the second most common attack vector behind traditional phishing (ESET H1 2025 Threat Report; Infosecurity Magazine, 2025). The technique exploits fake CAPTCHA/error messages to trick users into pasting malicious PowerShell commands via Win+R. Microsoft identified a May 2025 campaign specifically targeting Portuguese financial services organizations with Lampion banking malware via ClickFix (Microsoft Security Blog, August 2025). Sophos documented a complete **ClickFix → StealC → Qilin ransomware** attack chain where stolen VPN credentials were sold by an IAB approximately one month later (Sophos, "I am not a robot" blog, 2025). Nation-state actors including Iran's MuddyWater and Russia's APT28 adopted the technique (Logpoint; Proofpoint, 2025).

### Command and control, RMM abuse, and lateral movement

**Cobalt Strike** remains the most frequently observed C2 framework, now supplemented by **Sliver** (used by DEV-0237/FIN12 and APT29, per Microsoft/GCHQ), **Brute Ratel C4** (cracked versions on criminal forums at $2,500/license, per BleepingComputer/AdvIntel), and **Havoc** with its Demon agent supporting Microsoft Graph API integration for covert C2 (AlphaHunt, 2025–2026). Emerging tools include **GC2** (abusing Google Sheets as C2, observed in Fog ransomware operations) and **Adaptix** (Picus Security, 2025).

RMM tool abuse appeared in **36% of incident response cases**, with Arctic Wolf documenting malicious use of **32 different RMM tools** (Arctic Wolf 2025 Threat Report, via eSecurity Planet). **ConnectWise ScreenConnect** is the fastest-growing abuse vector: UNC5952 used signed malicious ScreenConnect droppers targeting global financial organizations (CyberProof, May 2025). **AnyDesk** was featured in ransomware activity by Mad Liberator, Medusa, Rhysida, and Cactus gangs (Intel 471, 2025), while Black Basta's 197,000 leaked chat messages confirmed systematic RMM abuse (Intel 471, February 2025). Akira installed **Datto RMM** on domain controllers to blend into routine IT automation (Barracuda, 2025).

**Credential harvesting** follows a predictable chain: Mimikatz for LSASS dumping (with sekurlsa::logonpasswords as the most-used module per Red Canary), Rubeus for Kerberoasting (documented in Akira's attack chain per Security Boulevard, November 2025), and DCSync attacks for KRBTGT hash extraction enabling Golden Ticket creation (Qualys ETM Defense Guide, February 2026). Median time to AD compromise from initial access: **11 hours** (Sophos, 2025 Active Adversary Report). Critically, **62% of compromised AD servers ran out-of-support operating systems** (Sophos, 2025).

### Exfiltration and evasion

**Rclone** is the dominant exfiltration tool, present in **57% of ransomware incidents** (ReliaQuest; Symantec/Broadcom; Infosecurity Magazine), used by LockBit, Black Basta, BlackSuit, Medusa, and others, typically uploading to MEGA.io accounts. WinSCP ranks second, followed by cURL (native to Windows 10 1803+), with FileZilla used by INC Ransom for FTP-based exfiltration (ReliaQuest). Exfiltration occurs at a **median of 72.98 hours** after attack initiation, with **83% of ransomware binaries deployed outside business hours** and 79% of exfiltration also occurring off-hours (Sophos, 2025). **96% of ransomware attacks in 2025 involved data exfiltration** alongside encryption (BlackFog Q3 2025, via Vectra AI).

**BYOVD (Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver)** attacks are the dominant evasion technique, with over **2,500 driver variants** used in a single campaign targeting the TrueSight driver (Vectra AI, 2025). **EDRKillShifter**, developed by RansomHub, is now used by **at least 8 distinct groups**: RansomHub, Medusa, BianLian, Play, BlackSuit, Qilin, DragonForce, and INC Ransom (ESET, March 2025; Arete; The Hacker News, March 2025). The cross-group sharing suggests active collaboration among typically closed RaaS operations; ESET identified the threat actor "QuadSwitcher" orchestrating cross-group attacks. The February 2026 Reynolds ransomware embedded a vulnerable driver directly in its payload, terminating CrowdStrike Falcon, Cortex XDR, Sophos, and Symantec (Vectra AI; Huntress, February 2026). In one notable case, Akira pivoted to an **unmonitored Linux-based webcam** on the same network after EDR quarantined the initial payload, encrypting the network from an agentless device (Vectra AI).

## 3. Vulnerabilities actively exploited with CISA KEV status

**Fortinet FortiGate** is the most frequently exploited edge device class, with **14 zero-day advisories in under 4 years** (Coalition Insurance). CVE-2024-55591 (CVSS 9.6, authentication bypass via Node.js websocket) was exploited as a zero-day since November 2024 with approximately **48,000 internet-facing devices** vulnerable (Shadowserver). Corvus Insurance confirmed "multiple ransomware groups have been actively exploiting this vulnerability" (Corvus Insurance advisory, February 2025). Added to CISA KEV January 14, 2025. In December 2025, CVE-2025-59718 and CVE-2025-59719 (CVSS 9.8, FortiCloud SSO bypass) triggered another wave: Arctic Wolf observed active intrusions within three days, Huntress reported 11 instances in 30 days (The Hacker News, December 2025; Help Net Security, January 21, 2026). CISA KEV added December 16, 2025. The January 2026 CVE-2026-24858 (cross-account SSO bypass) affected nearly **10,000 instances** (Shadowserver; CyberScoop, January 2026; CISA advisory, January 28, 2026). SentinelOne documented campaigns using FortiGate as entry points with pre-ransomware activity observed as of March 2026.

**Palo Alto Networks** CVE-2025-0108 (CVSS 8.8, PAN-OS authentication bypass) saw confirmed exploitation from February 18, 2025, chained with CVE-2024-9474 and CVE-2025-0111 for root-level access (Palo Alto Networks advisory; CSO Online, February 2025; Dark Reading). Chinese cyber-espionage group Emperor Dragonfly used Palo Alto exploits in RA World ransomware deployment (Symantec/Broadcom, February 2025). Added to CISA KEV February 2025.

**Veeam Backup & Replication** CVE-2024-40711 (CVSS 9.8, deserialization RCE) was exploited by Akira, Fog, and Frag ransomware variants, with Sophos X-Ops tracking 4+ incidents in October 2024 combining compromised VPN credentials with the Veeam vulnerability (Cybersecurity Dive, October 2024; Sophos X-Ops). Rapid7 noted **20%+ of their 2024 IR cases involved Veeam exploitation** (Rapid7, 2024). Added to CISA KEV October 17, 2024, marked "Known" for ransomware use. CVE-2025-23120 (CVSS 9.9) disclosed in March 2025 allows any authenticated domain user to execute code when the backup server is domain-joined (watchTowr; CSO Online).

**GoAnywhere MFT** CVE-2025-10035 (CVSS 10.0, command injection) was exploited as a zero-day by Storm-1175 (Medusa affiliate) since at least September 11, 2025, followed by Rclone exfiltration and ransomware encryption (SOCRadar, "Top 10 CVEs of 2025"; Microsoft attribution). Added to CISA KEV.

**Citrix NetScaler** CVE-2025-5777 ("CitrixBleed 2," CVSS 9.3) is critically relevant: Imperva observed 11.5 million exploitation attempts, with **40% targeting financial services** (Imperva; Tenable FAQ; Arctic Wolf; Cybersecurity Dive). One attacking IP was linked to RansomHub by CISA. Added to CISA KEV July 10, 2025.

**VMware ESXi** CVE-2025-22224/22225/22226 (VM escape chain, CVSS up to 9.3) were exploited as zero-days since at least February 2024 by Chinese-speaking threat actors. CISA confirmed CVE-2025-22225 is "Known To Be Used in Ransomware Campaigns" (February 2026 KEV update; Help Net Security, February 5, 2026; BleepingComputer; Broadcom advisory VMSA-2025-0004).

**Windows CLFS** CVE-2025-29824 was exploited by Storm-2460 targeting, among others, a **Venezuelan financial sector** entity and by Play ransomware-linked Balloonfly group (Microsoft Security Blog, April 8, 2025; Symantec/Security.com). Added to CISA KEV April 2025.

## 4. Supply chain compromises cascading through financial services vendors

**Supply chain attacks now account for 30–60% of all incidents**, depending on methodology, with financial services among the three most impacted sectors (Verizon DBIR 2025; SecurityScorecard, "2025 Global Third-Party Breach Report," March 26, 2025). SecurityScorecard found **41.4% of ransomware attacks start through third parties**, with Clop identified as the most prolific user of third-party access vectors. For fintech specifically, **41.8% of breaches** at top-250 companies originated from third-party vendors (SecurityScorecard, "Defending the Financial Supply Chain," May 21, 2025).

The **Clop Oracle EBS campaign** (CVE-2025-61882) was the defining supply chain event of 2025—compromising 100+ organizations running on-premise EBS installations from July through November 2025, exfiltrating payroll, financial ledgers, and HR files (Google Cloud Blog, October 2025; BlackFog, November 2025). The **Salesforce breach wave** represented a parallel paradigm: ShinyHunters (UNC6040/UNC6240) used vishing and malicious OAuth apps to compromise CRM instances at **Allianz Life Insurance** (~1.4 million U.S. customers), **TransUnion** (4.46 million individuals), and **Farmers Insurance** (~1.1 million policyholders) (TechTarget, 2025; Proven Data, "Biggest Data Breaches of 2025"; CaseyGerry, 2025). A separate supply chain vector through Salesloft's Drift chatbot OAuth integration compromised **700+ organizations** including Cloudflare, Zscaler, Palo Alto Networks, and Workday (Google Cloud Blog, August 2025).

Vendor-specific attacks directly targeting financial services clients included the **Marquis Software Solutions** attack (Akira, August 2025, 400,000+ consumers across 74+ banks), the **SitusAMC** breach (November 2025, affecting JPMorgan Chase, Citigroup, and Morgan Stanley per NYT/Bloomberg/CNN; CSO Online, November 2025), the **Prosper Marketplace** breach (17.6 million customers—the largest single financial services breach of 2025 by record count; Centraleyes, October 2025), and the **Insight Partners** ransomware attack ($90+ billion AUM PE firm, with 83-day dwell time; GBHackers, September 2025).

BitSight's analysis of 41,000+ financial organizations found that technology suppliers performed **worse than financial institutions in 16 of 22 risk categories**, with gaps up to 15%, and financial institutions monitor only **36.3% of their supply chain** for cyber risk (Help Net Security, November 11, 2025; BitSight, November 2025). IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach Report placed financial services breach costs at **$5.56 million** per incident (second highest after healthcare at $7.42M).

## 5. Infostealers and initial access brokers fueling the ransomware pipeline

The infostealer-to-ransomware pipeline has become the primary threat vector for financial institutions, with a **47% year-over-year increase** in infostealer attacks on financial institutions in 2025 (DeepStrike, "Stealer Log Statistics 2025") and **54% of ransomware extortion site victims** having domains appearing in at least one infostealer log (Verizon DBIR 2025).

**Lumma Stealer** was the most prevalent infostealer globally, accounting for **51% of all logs** on Russian dark web markets in November 2024 (Check Point, "2025 State of Cyber Security Report") and approximately 92% of credential log alerts on Russian Market (Bitsight, "2025 State of the Underground Report"; ReliaQuest). Microsoft identified **394,000 infected computers** from March–May 2025 before a global coalition seized 2,300+ domains in May 2025 (Microsoft Security Blog, May 21, 2025). Activity resumed within weeks, with targeted accounts returning to pre-takedown levels by June–July 2025 (Trend Micro, July 22, 2025). Ransomware groups including Octo Tempest, Storm-1607, Storm-1113, and Storm-1674 used Lumma-harvested credentials to breach targets (Microsoft Security Blog, May 2025).

**Vidar 2.0** released October 6, 2025, represents a complete C++ to C rewrite with **274 functions using control flow flattening**, Chrome AppBound encryption bypass, and Azure credential targeting (MSAL token cache, Azure CLI configs) at $300/month (Trend Micro, October 21, 2025; Ontinue analysis; SC Media). **StealC V2** (March 2025) was directly linked to ransomware when Sophos documented a ClickFix → StealC → Qilin attack chain, with stolen VPN credentials assessed (moderate confidence) as sold by an IAB (Sophos; Zscaler ThreatLabz, May 2025). **Rhadamanthys v0.9.2** introduced AI-powered OCR for extracting cryptocurrency seed phrases from images (Recorded Future, September 2024; Check Point Research, October 2025), before Operation Endgame Phase 2 seized 1,025 servers in November 2025 (SpyCloud; Europol). **Acreed**, a newcomer first observed in February 2025, surged after Lumma's takedown, uploading **118,000+ logs** to Russian Market by June 2025 and targeting SSO tokens from Microsoft 365, AWS, Azure, and Salesforce (Bitsight; GBHackers; Intrinsec).

**RedLine** and META were responsible for **64% of all infostealer-infected devices** in 2024 and stole over **451 million unique credentials** before Operation Magnus (October 28, 2024) seized backend infrastructure (Flashpoint; The Hacker News, October 2024; DOJ). Previous customers migrated to Lumma, Vidar, and StealC (Flashpoint).

The IAB marketplace shows financial services access averaging **$500–$3,000** per listing (Cyberint/Check Point, IAB Report, April 2025), with Flashpoint observing **6,406 posts** pertaining to financial sector access listings from April 2024 to April 2025 (Flashpoint, 2025). Session token theft now accounts for **31% of Microsoft 365 breaches** with approximately 40,000 daily token theft incidents across Microsoft environments (Obsidian Security, 2025). Tools like EvilGinx3 and Tycoon 2FA have industrialized adversary-in-the-middle token theft, with AiTM phishing attacks increasing **146%** (Obsidian Security).

## 6. Ransomware economics reveal shifting incentive structures

Financial services commands the **highest median ransom payment of any sector at $2.0 million** (Sophos, "State of Ransomware in Financial Services 2024"), with **51% of financial services victims paying** a ransom—though only 18% paid the full initial demand and firms paid an average of **75% of the initial ask** (Sophos Financial Services 2024). The sector's unique paradox: it has the **lowest encryption rate** (49%, down from 81% in 2023) reflecting stronger defenses, yet pays among the highest ransoms when forced to, reflecting extreme sensitivity to data exposure and regulatory consequences.

Payment rates are declining sharply across all sectors. Chainalysis recorded only **28% of victims paying** in 2025—a record low (Chainalysis, "2026 Crypto Crime Report," February 2026). Coveware tracked payment rates falling from 25% in Q4 2024 to approximately **20% by Q4 2025** (Coveware Q4 2024 Report, February 4, 2025; SOS Ransomware citing Coveware Q4 2025, February 2026). Coalition found **86% of businesses refused to pay** in 2025 (Coalition 2026 Cyber Claims Report, March 2026). Yet demands are rising: initial demands surged **47% year-over-year** (Coalition, 2026), and Chainalysis found median on-chain payments jumped **368% to ~$59,556** reflecting concentration on higher-value victims.

Total on-chain ransomware payments reached approximately **$820 million in 2025**, an 8% decline from 2024's revised $892 million (Chainalysis, 2026 Crypto Crime Report). FinCEN documented over **$2.1 billion in reported ransomware payments** in BSA filings from 2022–2024 (FinCEN, December 2025).

Recovery costs tell a stark story: mean recovery cost for financial services was **$2.58 million** excluding ransom (Sophos Financial Services 2024), while IBM placed the average financial services breach at **$5.56 million** (IBM Cost of a Data Breach Report 2025, August 2025). Financial firms with functional backups recovered for an average of **$375,000** versus **$3 million** for those who paid (Invenio IT analysis, updated 2026).

**Data-extortion-only attacks** are accelerating: encryption occurred in only **50% of ransomware attacks in 2025** (a six-year low), down from 70% in 2024 (Sophos, "State of Ransomware 2025"). However, Coveware found payment rates for data-extortion-only at just **19%**—victims increasingly doubt attackers will delete data (Coveware Q3 2025, via HIPAA Journal). Groups like Clop and World Leaks (formerly Hunters International) skip encryption entirely, while Qilin, Akira, and Play favor double extortion. Triple extortion now includes **SWATting** executives' homes and attempting to bribe employees (Coveware Q3 2025, via SOS Ransomware; HIPAA Journal). An emerging tactic: attackers are **stealing organizations' cyber insurance policies** to calibrate ransom demands (Resilience Midyear 2025 Cyber Risk Report, September 2025).

The cryptocurrency landscape shows Bitcoin remaining the primary payment rail (**97% of FinCEN-reported payments**), with bridge-related laundering growing **66%** while mixer activity declined **37%** as actors prefer cross-chain movement (TRM Labs 2026 Crypto Crime Report). OFAC designations in 2025 targeted the infrastructure layer: Zservers (February 11, LockBit hosting), AEZA Group (July 1), Garantex exchange successor Grinex ($100M+ illicit transactions, August), and Media Land LLC (November 19, supporting LockBit, BlackSuit, Play) (U.S. Treasury Press Releases).

## 7. Regulatory landscape intensifying across multiple jurisdictions

**SEC cybersecurity disclosure rules** (effective December 18, 2023) requiring 4-business-day materiality-based incident reporting on Form 8-K have reshaped the disclosure landscape: Wilson Sonsini tracked 55 incidents reported by 54 companies in the first year, though companies increasingly file under Item 8.01 (immaterial) rather than Item 1.05 (material) following SEC Corp Fin guidance (Wilson Sonsini/Known Trends, February 2025; SEC Corp Fin Director Gerding statement, May 21, 2024). The SEC's November 2025 voluntary dismissal of the SolarWinds case signals the Atkins-era SEC is backing away from aggressive cybersecurity enforcement, though the Division of Examinations 2026 Priorities (November 17, 2025) list cybersecurity governance, data loss prevention, access controls, and AI/polymorphic malware as examination focus areas (SEC Press Release 2025-132; Morrison Foerster; Jones Day; Cooley—all Q4 2025).

**FINRA's 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report** (December 9, 2025) places cybersecurity at the top of its risk list, calling out ransomware, imposter sites, and account takeovers. FINRA launched **CORE (Cyber & Operational REsilience)** in 2025 to collect and share cyber/technology risk intelligence with affected firms, and added a dedicated **GenAI section** for the first time (FINRA 2026 Annual Regulatory Oversight Report; analyses by McGuireWoods, Sidley Austin, December 2025). Third-party risk management is emphasized for the second consecutive year.

**CIRCIA final rule has been delayed to May 2026** (from original October 2025 target), per OMB's Spring 2025 Unified Agenda (Davis Wright Tremaine, September 2025; Inside Privacy, September 2025). CISA announced additional virtual town halls from March 9–April 2, 2026, with the **Financial Services Sector session scheduled for March 18, 2026** (CISA.gov; Wiley Law). The proposed rule would cover entities in all 16 critical infrastructure sectors exceeding SBA small business thresholds, requiring **72-hour incident reporting** and **24-hour ransomware payment reporting** for an estimated 300,000+ entities (Congressional Research Service R48025).

**NY DFS** has emerged as the most aggressive enforcement body, issuing **27 consent orders totaling over $144 million in fines** under Part 500 through Superintendent Adrienne Harris (NY DFS Press Release, October 14, 2025). Key actions include PayPal ($2M, January 2025), Block/Square ($40M, April 2025), and auto insurance companies ($19M+, October 2025). The November 1, 2025, final deadline mandated universal MFA for all information systems, complete asset inventories, and enhanced requirements for **Class A companies** (>$20M NY revenue AND >2,000 employees or >$1B global revenue) including independent audits, PAM, and EDR. A firm with this profile—52,000 employees and $1.8T AUM—would almost certainly qualify as a Class A company. Ransomware payments must be reported within **24 hours** with a 30-day follow-up analysis.

**EU DORA** became effective January 17, 2025, applying to 20+ types of financial entities with requirements spanning ICT risk management, incident reporting, resilience testing, and third-party oversight. Penalties reach **2% of annual worldwide turnover** (ESMA, EIOPA, EBA implementation guides). **OCC's Computer-Security Incident Notification Rule** requires banking organizations to report significant incidents to their primary federal regulator within **36 hours**, including ransomware encrypting core banking systems (ABA Banking Journal; OCC).

**FS-ISAC's "Navigating Cyber 2025" report** (May 19, 2025), drawing on 5,000+ member firms across 75 countries, found that while financial and insurance sectors accounted for approximately **8% of identified data leaks** from ransomware groups (4th most affected sector), demanded amounts increased, making 2024 "one of the highest-grossing years for ransomware operators" (FS-ISAC, May 2025). The sector's Cyber Threat Level remained at the **lowest of four levels**, reflecting the industry's ability to manage its threat landscape despite escalating external pressures.

## Conclusion: implications for a large wealth management firm

Five findings from this research stand out as particularly relevant to a firm of this profile.

First, **supply chain and third-party risk is the primary attack vector**, not direct compromise. The Marquis attack (74+ banks via a marketing vendor), Korean Leaks (28 firms via MSP), SitusAMC (JPMorgan/Citi/Morgan Stanley via mortgage tech vendor), and the Salesforce breach wave (Allianz Life/TransUnion via OAuth abuse) all demonstrate that adversaries target the weakest link in a financial firm's ecosystem. With 14,500 branch offices, the vendor footprint is enormous—yet BitSight found financial firms monitor only **36.3% of their supply chain** for cyber risk.

Second, **the infostealer-to-ransomware pipeline is now industrialized**. With 54% of ransomware victims having prior infostealer exposure, 6,406 financial sector access listings on IAB forums, and documented chains from ClickFix to StealC to Qilin deployment, credential hygiene and session token management are not just identity security concerns—they are ransomware prevention priorities.

Third, **edge device and VPN vulnerabilities represent existential risk** for a distributed branch network. Fortinet's 14 zero-days in four years, CitrixBleed 2 with 40% of exploitation targeting financial services, and Veeam backup destruction in 20%+ of IR cases create a vulnerability management challenge that exceeds what traditional patching cadences can address.

Fourth, **EDR evasion has matured dramatically**. EDRKillShifter's use by 8+ groups, the Reynolds ransomware embedding vulnerable drivers directly in payloads, and Akira's pivot to unmonitored IoT devices mean that EDR alone is insufficient—defense-in-depth including network segmentation, identity-based detection, and coverage of non-traditional endpoints is essential.

Fifth, **regulatory convergence creates both obligation and opportunity**. With NY DFS Class A requirements (highly likely applicable), SEC 8-K materiality determinations, FINRA CORE intelligence sharing, incoming CIRCIA reporting, and EU DORA obligations, the compliance overhead is substantial—but the underlying requirements (MFA everywhere, asset inventories, PAM, tested IR plans, third-party oversight) directly address the most exploited attack vectors documented in this report.
