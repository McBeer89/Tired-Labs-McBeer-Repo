# Top 10 Cybersecurity Writing Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

*Lenny Zeltser — SANS SEC402: Cybersecurity Writing: Hack the Reader*  
*Source: [youtube.com/watch?v=V7lO7UgxQV4](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=V7lO7UgxQV4)*

---

## The Key Principle

**Present your ideas on your reader's terms.** Develop empathy for your reader's expectations — understand what they want to see and how they want to see it.

---

## The Five Golden Elements

The 10 mistakes span five categories that form the golden elements of writing:

1. **Structure** — How you organize your content
2. **Look** — How your content appears visually
3. **Words** — How effective your language is
4. **Tone** — The feeling the reader gets from your writing
5. **Information** — What details you include or omit

---

## The 10 Mistakes

### 1. Burying the Main Point *(Structure)*

Don't make the reader work through supporting logic to reach your conclusion. Move the key takeaway to the front.

**Applies at every level:** the topic sentence of a paragraph, the heading of a section, and the executive summary of a report.

**Fix:** Preview your takeaways up front, in case the reader doesn't read the full text. You have their attention at the start — use it. Headings should be informative summaries of their sections. The executive summary must stand alone for readers who won't go further.

---

### 2. Overstuffing the Paragraphs *(Structure)*

Large paragraphs look overwhelming and uninviting. Readers are busy and impatient — they don't want to digest large chunks of information.

**Fix:** Split into easy-to-process chunks. Group related sentences together, then bring the summary sentence to the front of each chunk as a topic sentence. Splitting one paragraph into three gives the reader three topic sentences to skim instead of one.

**Process:** Jot down thoughts without worrying about structure. Group sentences containing related ideas. Bring the key idea to the front of each group as the topic sentence.

---

### 3. Including Indecipherable Graphics *(Look)*

Readers want to absorb the key idea of a graphic at a single glance. Pixelated tables, unlabeled charts, and meaningless visuals waste the reader's time and undermine credibility.

**Fix:**
- Choose chart types that communicate your main point effectively (bar charts over pie charts in most cases — humans judge relative sizes of bars far more easily than pie slices).
- Eliminate visual clutter and unnecessary formatting.
- Bring labels directly next to the data elements they describe.
- Add an informative title or caption — a full sentence stating the conclusion you want the reader to reach.
- Refer to every graphic from the body text so the reader knows how it fits the narrative.

---

### 4. Applying Inconsistent Formatting *(Look)*

Inconsistencies in spacing, heading styles, capitalization, fonts, and product name spelling all add up. They make your content look unprofessional and confuse readers because formatting is meant to be a visual guide through your content. If that guide is inconsistent, readers get misdirected.

**Fix:**
- Limit yourself to a couple of fonts and a couple of colors per document.
- Keep spacing between elements consistent (lines, paragraphs, headings, images).
- Use the style management features of your word processor (e.g., Microsoft Word Styles) instead of manually formatting every element.
- Be especially careful with copy-pasted content — it may carry conflicting formatting from the source.
- Maintain uniform capitalization conventions for headings and product names throughout.

---

### 5. Using More Words Than Necessary *(Words)*

Readers don't have time or patience for unnecessary words. Every extra word costs the reader energy that they won't have left for the rest of your content.

**Fix:** Challenge yourself to shorten every paragraph by at least 20%. Scrutinize each word — if its absence doesn't detract from meaning, cut it. Get to the point faster.

**"When in doubt, cut it out."**

Be brief, but don't cut details your readers actually need. Include just the words you need, but don't forget to supply the information your readers expect.

---

### 6. Not Using Parallel Structure *(Words)*

Readers want consistent patterns for easier skimming. When elements in a list, series, or set of headings follow different grammatical forms, it slows the reader's ability to comprehend.

**Fix:** Every element in a series should follow the same grammatical form. If your bullet points start with verbs, every bullet point starts with a verb in the same tense.

**Applies to:** bulleted lists, series of words within a sentence, and section headings in a report.

---

### 7. Using FUD to Cause Anxiety *(Tone)*

Fear, uncertainty, and doubt (FUD) should only appear in your writing when describing specific, realistic, relevant risks with associated mitigation approaches.

**The distinction:** Fear is a reaction to a specific threat that has a countermeasure. Anxiety is a general state of distress. FUD creates anxiety, which scares people into inaction — the opposite of what you want.

**Fix:** Focus on specific risk factors relevant to your readers. Propose a plan to mitigate every risk you raise. Avoid indiscriminately raising uncertainty and doubt. Let the facts convey urgency — if the threat is real, you don't need hyperbole.

---

### 8. Escalating Tensions in Difficult Situations *(Tone)*

Aggressive, confrontational writing is counterproductive even when your concerns are well-founded and urgent. Readers want interactions that are constructive, not confrontational.

**Fix:**
- Remain tactful and courteous in a professional context.
- Criticize the action, not the person.
- Discuss the negative consequences to the organization — don't assume the reader understands why you're unhappy.
- Clarify what steps you'd like taken to improve the situation. If you don't, readers will ignore the message or take the wrong action.

---

### 9. Including Details Most Readers Don't Need *(Information)*

Content that most readers don't need distracts from the content that is truly important. It costs readers time, energy, and patience.

**Fix:** Focus your reader's attention on the details they need. Anticipate the questions your readers will ask and answer those.

- Details that might be relevant to some readers but not most belong in an **appendix**, not the body.
- Always refer to the appendix from the body text so readers know it exists and how it fits the narrative.
- Use informative headings so different audiences can navigate to what they need. This is how you speak to multiple audiences with a single report.

---

### 10. Not Giving Credit Where Credit Is Due *(Information)*

"It has been determined" — by whom? "Researchers identified" — which researchers? Vague attribution prevents the reader from judging credibility.

**Fix:**
- Clarify what you derived yourself and what came from others.
- Include references to anyone you're quoting or paraphrasing. This lets the reader understand the building blocks from which you arrived at your conclusions.
- Attribute work to specific people, teams, or organizations so the reader can assess trustworthiness.
- Thank contributors by name — genuine gratitude goes a long way and adds a positive element to what is often a negative message.
